# SKILL: Freelance & Self-Employed Tax Set-Aside Calculator

## When to use this skill
Use this skill when the user asks:
- How much should I set aside for taxes on freelance income?
- I made $X consulting/freelancing — what do I owe?
- How do I handle HST/GST as a self-employed person in Canada?
- What's my quarterly tax estimate?
- I have a side hustle — how does this affect my taxes?

---

## Step-by-step reasoning process

### Step 1 — Determine the nature of the income
Ask or confirm:
- Is this the user's only income, or do they also have employment income?
- What province are they in?
- Have they registered for HST/GST? (Required once revenue exceeds $30,000 in a calendar year)

### Step 2 — Calculate gross self-employment income
Use the figure the user provided. This is their revenue before expenses.

### Step 3 — Estimate deductible business expenses
Common eligible deductions for Canadian self-employed:
- Home office (if dedicated space): % of rent/mortgage × business use %
- Internet and phone: business-use portion
- Equipment and software
- Professional development, courses, books
- Vehicle (if used for business): business km / total km × vehicle costs
- Professional fees (accountant, lawyer)

Rule of thumb if user has no specific expenses: assume 15–20% of revenue in expenses for knowledge workers.

Adjusted income = Gross revenue − Business expenses

### Step 4 — Calculate CPP contributions
Self-employed Canadians pay **both the employee and employer portions of CPP**.
- 2025 CPP contribution rate: 11.9% of net self-employment income
- 2025 CPP maximum pensionable earnings: $71,300
- 2025 basic exemption: $3,500
- Maximum CPP contribution for self-employed (2025): ~$8,068

Formula:
> CPP = (net self-employment income − $3,500) × 11.9%
> Cap at $8,068

### Step 5 — Calculate federal + provincial income tax
Add self-employment income to any other income to get total income.
Use combined marginal rates from the province (see RRSP vs TFSA skill for rate table).

For a rough set-aside estimate:
- Income $50,000–$100,000: set aside ~28–33% of net income
- Income $100,000–$150,000: set aside ~38–43% of net income
- Income $150,000+: set aside ~45–50% of net income

### Step 6 — Calculate HST/GST owing (if registered)
- Federal HST rate in ON: 13%
- HST is collected from clients and remitted to CRA — it is NOT your income.
- If the user collects HST: set it aside completely in a separate account. Do not spend it.
- HST remittance schedule: quarterly if annual revenue < $1.5M.

### Step 7 — Build the set-aside recommendation
Present clearly:

> **On $X of freelance revenue:**
> - Set aside for income tax: $Y (~Z%)
> - Set aside for CPP: $A
> - Set aside for HST (if applicable): $B
> - **Total to set aside: $C**
> - **Amount you keep: $D**

Recommend they open a **separate savings account** for the tax set-aside and treat it as untouchable.

## Key rules to always mention if relevant
- CRA instalments are required if you owe more than **$3,000 in taxes** in the current AND either of the two prior years. Instalments are due March, June, September, December.
- Self-employed individuals have until **June 15** to file their return (but any balance owing is still due April 30 — interest accrues after that).
- The **business-use-of-home deduction** cannot create or increase a business loss — it can only reduce income to zero.
- Keep all receipts for **6 years** — CRA's standard audit window.
- HST input tax credits (ITCs) allow you to reclaim HST paid on business expenses — make sure to claim them.
